Existentialism According to Nietzsche

By Isaac Maldonado Published June 15, 2023 7 min read

In this essay we explore how Nietzsche revolutionized existentialist thought with concepts like the Übermensch and the death of God, laying foundations for atheistic existentialism.

Historical Context

Nietzsche developed his ideas during late 19th century Europe's intellectual upheaval. His proclamation "God is dead" marked the collapse of absolute morality, forcing humanity to confront existential questions: Without divine purpose, where does meaning come from?

The Übermensch Concept

The Übermensch (Overman) represents Nietzsche's ideal of self-created values beyond good/evil. This directly influenced:

  • Sartre's "existence precedes essence"
  • Camus' absurd hero in The Myth of Sisyphus
  • Heidegger's authenticity in Being and Time

Nietzsche's Influence on Atheistic Existentialism

Key Impacts:

Martin Heidegger

Adapted Nietzsche's critique of metaphysics while analyzing "Being-toward-death" as authentic existence.

Jean-Paul Sartre

Expanded radical freedom concepts in Being and Nothingness (1943), declaring humans "condemned to be free."

Albert Camus

The absurd hero mirrors Nietzsche's amor fati - loving one's fate despite cosmic meaninglessness.

The Death of God and Its Consequences

Nietzsche's most radical proposition dismantled:

  1. Traditional morality's foundations
  2. Metaphysical comfort in afterlife
  3. Objective meaning systems
"We have killed him - you and I. How shall we comfort ourselves, the murderers of all murderers?" - The Gay Science (1882)
